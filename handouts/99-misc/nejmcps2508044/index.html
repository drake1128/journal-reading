A 47-year-old man was transported to the emergency department (ED) by emergency medical services (EMS) after being found dyspneic in his car. He reported a history of drinking 4 to 6 alcoholic beverages per day and stated that he had been drinking alcohol for 6 hours before presentation. During the previous 2 hours, he had had a sensation of tongue swelling, along with difficulty breathing. He reported no headache, sore throat, odynophagia, nausea, vomiting, rash, itching, or swelling of the face or limbs. He stated that he had a penicillin allergy. He reported no recent use of medications, consumption of new foods, or insect bites. He had had multiple ED visits for alcohol intoxication over the course of the previous 2 years (with the most recent visit occurring 10 days before presentation); he also had a history of chronic thrombocytopenia and of episodes of delirium tremens. He reported that he was taking no medications on a long-term basis. Other social history was limited owing to his acute condition, but previous documenta- tion from a social worker indicated that he was unemployed and living with his mother locally in the southeastern United States. On arrival at the ED, the patient had a blood pressure of 139/102 mm Hg, a pulse of 104 beats per minute, a respiratory rate of 22 breaths per minute, and an oxygen satura- tion of 91% while he was breathing ambient air. He was afebrile but diaphoretic, and he was awake and alert although intermittently inattentive. He had slurred, muffled speech but could speak full sentences without stridor or cough. No facial edema or tongue swelling was present. The lungs were clear on auscultation, and the abdomen was soft and nontender. En route to the ED, he had received treatment with epinephrine, diphen- hydramine, and glucocorticoids, but his symptoms did not abate. The differential diagnosis of dyspnea is broad, but the associated muffled speech implies an upper-airway process. The initiation of treatment for possible anaphylaxis or angioedema was appropriate. However, the patient has no tongue or other swelling that would suggest angioedema, and he has no urticaria, flushing, gastrointestinal symptoms, or hypotension that would be suggestive of anaphylaxis. In addition, the rate of symptom progression (over a period of hours) and the lack of a reduction in symptoms with epinephrine would be atypical for anaphylaxis. Oropharyngeal infec- tions such as peritonsillar abscesses, infections of the deep neck spaces, and acute epiglottitis should be considered, but the absence of fever, odynophagia, and sore throat argue against such infections. Acute alcohol intoxication can cause dysarthria and impaired airway protection; however, the patient could speak coherently in com- plete sentences during the initial assessments. Chest radiography revealed low lung volumes and faint bibasilar opacities. The otolaryngology service was consulted for fiberoptic laryngoscopy, which revealed limited abduction of the vocal cords during phonation, with no tongue or airway swell- ing. The patient received treatment with racemic epinephrine, an additional intramuscular 0.3-mg dose of epinephrine, methylprednisolone (at a dose of 125 mg), and intramuscular thiamine (at a dose of 500 mg). Initial CT of the head showed no intracranial ab- normalities, and CT venography and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) of the head did not show venous sinus thrombosis, a mass lesion, or abnor- mal enhancement. No abnormalities were ob- served on diffusion-weighted sequences. The pa- tient’s condition stabilized after pressure-support ventilation was initiated; the fraction of inspired oxygen was 0.40, and the oxygen saturation was 97%. He remained hypertensive, with a blood pres- sure of 160/90 mm Hg. The absence of oropharyngeal edema makes anaphylaxis and angioedema unlikely, and the presence of a normal glottis is inconsistent with epiglottitis. The vocal-cord paresis noted on la- ryngoscopy explains his abnormal speech and increases his risk of oropharyngeal aspiration, which may underlie his respiratory failure. Oph- thalmoplegia of both eyes points to an underlying neurologic cause. Many central neurologic causes are unlikely; the normal results on imaging of the head argue against cancer, vascular disease, and inflammatory disease, and the patient has no headache, fever, or neck stiffness that would sug- gest meningitis. Neuroborreliosis or neurosyphi- lis can affect the cranial nerves, but vocal-cord paresis would be expected to be a late finding; moreover, the patient does not live in a region in which Lyme disease is endemic. Other possible central causes of ophthalmople- gia include Wernicke’s encephalopathy and Bicker- staff’s brain-stem encephalitis. In addition to ophthalmoplegia, hallmarks of Wernicke’s en- cephalopathy include ataxia and confusion, which were not apparent — but were also difficult to assess — in this intoxicated patient. Bickerstaff’s brain-stem encephalitis is an inflammatory disor- der that may be manifested by ataxia, headache, seizures, altered mentation, various cranial-nerve deficits (including ophthalmoplegia), or a combi- nation of these conditions. Both Wernicke’s en- cephalopathy and Bickerstaff’s brain-stem en- cephalitis lead to vocal-cord paresis and dysphagia in rare cases. Of these possible causes, only the Guillain– Barré syndrome is typically associated with trans- aminitis, although it is usually a mild and transient form, whereas this patient has aminotransfer- ase levels that are more consistent with alcohol- associated disease or cirrhosis. In the intensive care unit (ICU), the patient received treatment with thiamine and folate, given the con- cern about Wernicke’s encephalopathy, and was monitored for alcohol withdrawal, given his history of delirium tremens. The neurology service was consulted, and a comprehensive neurologic ex- amination that was performed without sedation showed that he could open his eyes on command and could nod or shake his head appropriately to answer questions. The pupils were reactive and equal, but he could not move his eyes vertically or laterally; his eyes also did not move with passive head rotation. The corneal reflexes were intact, as was the cough reflex. Intermittent tremulous move- ments were noted in both forearms. Motor strength was at least 3/5 in all four limbs. Sensation to light touch was intact. Reflexes were 2+ in the biceps, brachioradialis, and triceps of both arms; the patel- lae; and the ankles. The Babinski reflex and Hoff- mann’s sign were absent. An examination of the cerebellum was not performed. Wernicke’s encephalopathy is primarily a clinical diagnosis; early MRI can have low sensitivity. Given the favorable side-effect profile of thiamine coupled with the risk of progression of untreated Wernicke’s encephalopathy to Korsakoff’s syn- drome (with potentially irreversible cognitive im- pairment), thiamine (at a dose of 200 to 500 mg per day for up to 7 days) should be administered when clinical suspicion is high. Treatment is pru- dent in this patient with ophthalmoplegia and alcohol use disorder, although the examination findings do not suggest encephalopathy. Peripheral nervous system processes such as the Guillain– Barré syndrome and botulism remain important considerations in the differential diagnosis of oph- thalmoplegia. Further workup, including a lumbar puncture, is warranted. The next day, while the patient was not under se- dation, he no longer opened his eyes on command or withdrew in response to noxious stimuli. An electroencephalogram showed continuous low- amplitude alpha activity. Later that evening, a fever with a temperature of up to 39.7°C developed, along with tachycardia (heart rate, 130 beats per minute), leukocytosis (white-cell count, 14,000 per cubic millimeter), and worsening hypoxemia (fraction of inspired oxygen, 0.70). Repeat chest radiography revealed consolidation in both lower lobes. The C-reactive protein level was 16 mg per liter (normal value, <10), and the lactate level was 2.6 mmol per liter (23 mg per deciliter). The patient received treat- ment with ceftriaxone, but hypotension subsequent- ly developed (blood pressure, 86/60 mm Hg). Given concern about possible ceftriaxone-mediated ana- phylaxis, intramuscular epinephrine, methylpred- nisolone (at a dose of 125 mg), and vasopressors were administered, and treatment with ceftriaxone was switched to levofloxacin and metronidazole. The patient’s new fever and hypotension are most likely due to sepsis resulting from aspiration pneu- monia. The electroencephalogram shows a normal posterior-dominant rhythm, as occurs in a con- scious patient with closed eyes, that is inconsistent with encephalopathy. Allergic reactions to third- generation cephalosporins are uncommon, even among patients who have had anaphylaxis to penicillin. Alternatively, autonomic dysfunction, as occurs in patients with botulism or LEMS, may explain this patient’s hypotension. Although the Guillain–Barré syndrome can also cause auto- nomic dysfunction, it is typically associated with episodes of both hypotension and hypertension. He did not withdraw in response to nox- ious stimuli, but spontaneous toe movement was observed. Motor reflexes were now absent through- out; no pathologic reflexes were noted. The worsening descending paralysis, despite the administration of thiamine, and new areflexia (rather than hyperreflexia) favor a peripheral ner- vous system disease over a central process. In patients with the Guillain–Barré syndrome, the presence of albuminocytologic dissociation (an elevated level of protein in the CSF without pleo- cytosis) is common, often with extremely elevated protein levels. The near-normal CSF results seen in this patient are more suggestive of botulism, my- asthenia gravis, or LEMS. Botulism was now the leading diagnosis. The pa- tient’s mother, who first arrived at the hospital on the morning of hospital day 3, stated that the patient often ate perishable foods that had been left at room temperature for several days. The state health department and the Centers for Disease Con- trol and Prevention (CDC) were contacted to request botulism antitoxin, and stool and serum samples were obtained and sent to the CDC for botulinum neurotoxin testing. The patient received the anti- toxin 90 hours after presentation in the ED. Electro- myography and nerve-conduction studies that were performed in the medical ICU 48 hours after ad- ministration of the antitoxin showed the absence of motor-unit action potentials throughout his body, but sensory-nerve action potentials were found to be intact. Repetitive nerve stimulation showed nei- ther a decremental response to slow rates of stimu- lation nor a substantive incremental response to high rates of stimulation. Given the concern that the acute motor axonal neuropathy (AMAN) subtype of the Guillain–Barré syndrome, which involves mo- tor axons only, remained a possible alternative ex- planation for the patient’s presentation, empirical treatment with intravenous immune globulin (at a dose of 2 g per kilogram of body weight) was also administered. However, no neurologic improve- ment was noted. A classic finding on electromyography and nerve- conduction studies in patients with botulism is an incremental increase in the amplitude of the compound muscle action potential in response to high frequencies on repetitive nerve stimula- tion. However, this finding is absent in some cases, and this patient’s exposure history is con- sistent with foodborne botulism. The AMAN subtype of the Guillain–Barré syndrome could also explain the findings on electromyography and nerve-conduction studies; however, the pres- ence of marked cranial-nerve involvement and the absence of both albuminocytologic dissocia- tion and a response to intravenous immune globu- lin makes this diagnosis unlikely. The absence of changes in response to repetitive nerve stimula- tion and the lack of improvement after intrave- nous immune globulin and glucocorticoid thera- py argues against myasthenia gravis and LEMS. Although empirical treatment for botulism was appropriate, the delay between the patient’s pre- sentation and receipt of the antitoxin was sub- stantial; irreversible loss of function is possible. The results of the patient’s repeat neurologic ex- amination remained unchanged. Fever and hypo- tension resolved with antibiotic agents. The serum sample that had been analyzed by the CDC by means of mass spectrometry (Endopep-MS assay) was positive for botulinum neurotoxin type A. Neu- rotoxin was not detected in the stool sample, and the results of the mouse bioassay were equivocal. However, the stool sample had arrived at the CDC at a temperature that was outside of standard storage temperatures, which may have led to false negative results. GM1 antibody testing was negative. Testing for botulinum neurotoxin is highly spe- cific, and a positive result confirms the diagnosis of botulism. Negative GM1 antibody testing fur- ther reduces the likelihood of the Guillain–Barré syndrome, including the AMAN subtype. The patient remained dependent on mechanical ventilation and underwent tracheostomy. Once his condition was medically stable, he was discharged to a long-term acute care facility. One month after diagnosis, a neurologic examination showed 0/5 strength in both arms and hands, except for 1/5 strength in finger flexion and extension. Strength was 1/5 in flexion and extension of both hips and both knees and 3/5 in dorsiflexion and plantar flex- ion of both feet. After 3 months of rehabilitation after the administration of the antitoxin, the patient still had not regained voluntary function beyond movement of his feet. His botulism was probably foodborne, given his history of consuming perishable foods left at room temperature for several days. Unfortunately, his history of alcohol use disorder delayed his di- agnosis, and the antitoxin was administered only after diaphragmatic paralysis had developed. Botulism is a rare and potentially fatal disor- der caused by botulinum neurotoxin. This neuro- toxin is produced by Clostridium botulinum, a spore- forming, anaerobic, gram-positive bacterium found in soil, dust, and aquatic sediment. Botulinum neurotoxin has seven serotypes (types A through G), all of which cause paralysis by irreversibly in- hibiting acetylcholine release at the neuromuscular junction of voluntary motor and autonomic neu- rons (Fig. 2). Botulism can be caused by preformed toxin, as with foodborne and iatrogenic botulism (after cosmetic or therapeutic injections), or by lo- cal production, as with wound botulism, intestinal colonization in adults, and infant botulism (infant intestinal colonization).1-3 Foodborne botulism in adults is uncommon, with only 326 cases reported in the United States between 2001 and 2017.4 Despite its classic asso- ciation with foodborne botulism, improper home food canning accounted for only one third of these cases.4 Toxin production can occur in foods with low acidity, a low salt content, or a low sugar con- tent that are contaminated with viable C. botulinum spores and have been left at ambient temperatures in low-oxygen conditions.2 Cases involving con- sumption of chili and nacho cheese that had been packaged in plastic and improperly stored have been reported.4 Botulism is classically described as a symmet- ric descending motor paralysis. However, symp- toms vary in severity (from subclinical to fatal) and in rate of progression depending on the neu- rotoxin serotype and exposure quantity.1-3,5 Symp- toms typically begin 2 hours to 8 days after expo- sure to the neurotoxin, with a peak at 48 hours.1,3,6 In a large series of cases of botulism that led to hospitalization (86% of which were foodborne),6 symmetric cranial-nerve palsies were often the initial symptom, manifesting as difficulties with vision or eye movement (e.g., diplopia, ptosis, vi- sion blurriness, and ocular palsy) or bulbar weak- ness (e.g., dysphagia, dysarthria, and dysphonia). General weakness, vomiting, nausea, dry mouth, and abdominal pain were also present in many cases. Respiratory distress was noted on admis- sion in one third of the cases. Without treatment, symptoms can progress to symmetric limb motor weakness and neuromuscular respiratory failure resulting from diaphragmatic weakness.3,6 In some cases, cranial-nerve palsies contribute to respira- tory failure resulting from pharyngeal collapse and airway compromise.3 There is usually no loss of sensation or consciousness. If fever is present, it is most likely explained by a superimposed infection. The differential diagnosis of symmetric de- scending motor paralysis or symmetric cranial nerve palsies (or both) is limited and includes the Guillain–Barré syndrome, LEMS, myasthenia gra- vis, Bickerstaff’s brain-stem encephalitis, viral encephalitis, a severe myopathy, and paralytic shellfish poisoning.3 In the absence of shellfish exposure, the occurrence of clusters of cases of symmetric descending motor paralysis strongly suggests botulism. The diagnosis of botulism is confirmed by the detection of neurotoxin in se- rum and stool with the use of a combination of mouse bioassays and molecular tests.7 Although these assays are highly specific, their sensitivity is dependent on the timing of the sample collection relative to the onset of symptoms and administra- tion of the antitoxin, as well as sample storage, the cause of the poisoning, and absorption and metabolism kinetics of the neurotoxin in the pa- tient’s body.8,9 Botulinum neurotoxin has been detected in only 70% of samples obtained from patients with clinical foodborne botulism.8 Elec- tromyography and nerve-conduction studies can be helpful because they often show small com- pound muscle action potentials and incremental increases in amplitudes with high-frequency re- petitive nerve stimulation.1 However, as in this case, some patients lack these findings, especially those with severe disease and those exposed to botulinum neurotoxin type A.10 Treatment of botulism includes the administra- tion of the equine-derived heptavalent botulinum The New England Journal of Medicine is produced by NEJM Group, a division of the Massachusetts Medical Society. (The CDC also coordinates epidemiologic investigations and con- firmatory testing.) Administration of the antitoxin more than 48 hours after the onset of symptoms has been associated with longer ICU and hospital stays and decreased survival.5,11 Adverse reactions to the antitoxin are uncommon and generally self- limited but include a risk of anaphylaxis (reported to occur in approximately 1.4% of patients13); epi- nephrine should be available at the time of admin- istration. Supportive critical care, which may include weeks to months of mechanical ventilation, is a cornerstone of management of severe cases of botulism; advancements in ventilator technology have been associated with reduced mortality.2,3,14 Nevertheless, mortality remains as high as 10%, even among patients who receive the antitoxin, often owing to respiratory failure or sequelae of paralysis.6,11,14 Patients who survive often recover normal ventilatory muscle strength within 1 year, but fatigue, exercise intolerance, general weakness, dyspnea, and dry mouth can persist.2,3 This case underscores the need to consider botulism in the differential diagnosis for any pa- tient presenting with acute-onset ophthalmoplegia and dysarthria and the critical importance of early administration of the antitoxin to reduce the risk of death and complications.
