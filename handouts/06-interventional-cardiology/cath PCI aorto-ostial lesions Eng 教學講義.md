## Angiographic Views for Ostial Disease


### Principles of Optimal Ostial Visualization


Obtaining a coronary angiogram in a patient with aorto-ostial disease is one of the most challenging angiographic tasks, both in terms of properly delineating the ostial stenosis and managing catheter-induced ischemia and damping.

The key principle is to obtain a view that lays out the ostium junction with the cusp and the aorta as **one line** in a two-dimensional angle. You do NOT want the cusp overlying the ostium, which obscures proper delineation.


### Standard Views by Vessel


| p{5cm}p{5cm}} Optimal Angiographic Views for Ostial Disease |  |  |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Vessel** | **Recommended Views** | **Views to Avoid** |
| \tablename  -- continued |  |  |
| **Vessel** | **Recommended Views** | **Views to Avoid** |
| **Ostial Left Main** | LAO 30° + Cranial 20-30°    LAO straight    AP cranial | LAO caudal (superimposes cusp over ostium)    Caudal views in general |
| **Ostial RCA** | Steep LAO 40-50°    ± Cranial or Caudal angulation | Standard LAO 30° (cusp not well separated) |
| **SVG to RCA** | LAO (orthogonal to graft takeoff) | — |
| **SVG to LCA** | RAO (orthogonal to graft takeoff) | — |


### Special Considerations


- **Anterior takeoff RCA**: Requires even steeper LAO (or steep RAO) to open the ostium
- **CT analysis**: Studies show optimal orthogonal views are:     itemize
- LCA: LAO cranial
- RCA: Steep LAO with cranial angulation (though LAO caudal may better show the very proximal portion)


    \item **Practical limitation**: Very steep LAO increases soft tissue penetration and image blur
    \item **Marker rings**: If present around graft ostium, obtain view where the circle becomes one line
itemize


## Damping and Ventricularization


### Definitions and Recognition


| p{10cm}} Catheter Pressure Waveform Changes |  |  |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Condition** | **Description** |  |
| \tablename  -- continued |  |  |
| **Condition** | **Description** |  |
| **Normal Engagement** | Aortic pressure waveform transmitted at catheter tip |  |
| **Damping** | Catheter wedges the artery; pressure waveform becomes blunted with reduced pulse pressure |  |
| **Mild Ventricularization** | Segment between peaks becomes horizontal, sometimes with small a-wave |  |
| **Severe Ventricularization** | Pressure at tip reflects downstream ventricular pressure; looks almost like true LV pressure |  |
| **Complete Occlusion** | Flat line - catheter against wall of ostium or against plaque |  |


**NEVER inject contrast while damped or ventricularized!**

- Contrast will hang in the coronary and not clear
- Further aggravates ischemia already created by the catheter
- **Myocardial staining** = contrast staining the actual myocardium = **1 second away from VF**
- Risk of coronary dissection from high-pressure injection


**Action**: Immediately disengage the catheter when damping/ventricularization is noted!


## Imaging Techniques with Damped Catheter


When your catheter damps or ventricularizes upon engagement, there are four techniques to obtain diagnostic images:


### Technique 1: Barely Disengaged Position (Preferred)


- Disengage the catheter to be "barely non-selective"
- Inject while catheter is just slightly out
- Good images without occlusive or ventricularized waveform
- **For RCA**: Counter-clockwise torque (opposite to engagement) keeps catheter at same level but slightly out
- **For LCA**: Slight clockwise or counter-clockwise torque, or gentle pullback
- Consider using a shorter-tip catheter (e.g., JL instead of Tiger or EBU)


### Technique 2: Hit-and-Run Injection


- Quickly inject 1-2 cc (RCA) or 4-5 cc (LCA) while damped/ventricularized
- Immediately withdraw catheter after injection
- **Limitations**:     itemize
- Still risk of dissection
- Need to re-engage multiple times for complete assessment
- Risk of myocardial staining


itemize


### Technique 3: Imaging While Mildly Ventricularized


- Only applicable if **mildly** ventricularized (not severely)
- May take a couple of quick images
- Not recommended for severe ventricularization


### Technique 4: Hit-and-Run Wiring (Most Recommended)


**Hit-and-Run Wiring Technique** - The most "stylish" technique for interventionalists:

1. Advance guide to aorta; have wire ready inside guide before engagement
2. Engage coronary (accept damping, but do NOT inject)
3. Quickly wire the coronary blindly while damped
4. Disengage guide - wire anchors the guide preventing fly-out
5. Obtain images in stable, barely disengaged position
6. Consider using **iFR/FFR wire** to accomplish imaging AND physiologic assessment simultaneously


**Important**: When doing ostial iFR/FFR, always disengage the guide during measurements. Engaged guide causes falsely high iFR values because obstruction limits flow drop.


## PCI Techniques with Damped Guide


### Hit-and-Run Maneuvering of Balloon and Stent


The same principles of hit-and-run wiring apply to stenting:


1. Wire while damped, then disengage to obtain images
2. Decide to balloon/stent
3. Advance stent and position inside guide while guide is disengaged (no ischemia)
4. Engage (accept damping) to get support
5. Advance stent to target while damped
6. Disengage catheter to relieve ischemia
7. Deploy stent while barely disengaged


**Tip**: For severe left main disease, balloon the LM first to allow patient to better tolerate subsequent ischemia during maneuvers.


### Stenting While Barely Disengaged


If the guide can maintain a stable barely-disengaged position:

- Wire and stent while guide is partially out
- No hit-and-run needed
- Applicable mainly for ostial stenting (not for distal disease requiring deep engagement for support)


## Side-Hole Guide Catheters: Why NOT to Use


**Side-hole guides should be avoided in most ostial PCI cases.**

**Five Reasons Against Side-Hole Guides**:

1. Flow provided is marginal (few percent of normal) - does NOT markedly attenuate ischemia
2. Improves waveform by transmitting aortic pressure through side holes, NOT by relieving ischemia - provides **false sense of security**
3. Loss of damping as a diagnostic tool - damping tells you when guide needs to be disengaged during stent positioning
4. **NEVER** use in left main PCI - you don't want concealed LM ischemia
5. No value in ostial RCA PCI - should be mostly disengaged anyway


**Only potential exception**: Mid-to-distal RCA PCI through moderate ostial disease/small ostium that you don't need to stent, in a patient with small RCA territory. May reduce risk of dye stain and VF.

**Transradial RCA Ostial Stenting with JR4 - The Problem:**

- Excessive catheter motion with respiration, especially in obese patients
- With each deep breath, the catheter may fly out to the aorta
- Nightmare scenario: Stent partially deployed flies into aorta with deep breath


**Solution**: Use Amplatz Left in elongated fashion (not duck-shaped), AR2, or Ikari catheters which anchor better and resist respiratory motion.


## Stent Positioning: Avoiding Geographic Miss


### The Anatomy of Aorto-Ostial Junction


CT studies show stent is misplaced in approximately 50


> **The lower edge is more forward than the upper edge:**
> itemize[leftmargin=*]
>     \item **Lower edge**: At the cusp level
>     \item **Upper edge**: Close to tubular aorta
> itemize
> **Perfect stent positioning:**
> itemize[leftmargin=*]
>     \item Position stent a couple of millimeters proximal to the **lower edge**
>     \item You **MUST** cover the lower edge into the cusp
>     \item You do **NOT** need to cover the upper edge
>     \item Blood flows through the aorta and cusp into the "chimney" created by the stent
>     \item Use stent length $\geq$12 mm for ostial lesions
> itemize


**This is DIFFERENT from branch ostial disease:**

- In branch ostial disease, you MUST cover the upper carina
- In aorto-ostial disease, covering the upper edge may be harmful (stent extending too far into aorta)


### Ostial Flaring Technique


After deploying the ostial stent:

1. Pull back the stent balloon
2. Inflate at **higher pressure** with balloon partially hanging in the aorta
3. This "flares" the proximal stent to:      - Make future engagement easier - Address the high elastic recoil at the ostium - Create a tapered appearance into the aorta


**Ostial Flush Balloon**: A specialized double-balloon catheter with a proximal spherical balloon for symmetrical flaring. Standard balloon works well too.


## Hand Maneuvers for Precise Stent Positioning


### The Three-Element Problem


In any intervention, you must watch three elements: the **guide**, the **wire**, and the **stent**. Each action on one causes an opposite reaction on the other two.

**The Ostial Stenting Challenge**:

- Pull stent back to ostium $\rightarrow$ Guide and wire dive in
- Pull guide back to uncover ostium $\rightarrow$ Stent dives further in
- This cycle repeats, preventing proper positioning


### The Palm of the Left Hand Technique


**Master this technique for successful ostial stenting:**

**Step 1 - Positioning the Stent:**

- Right hand: Pull the stent to position it at the ostium
- Left hand palm: Simultaneously pull back tension on the guide
- Left hand fingers: Pull back on the wire (prevent perforation)
- Watch the screen - adjust tension to keep guide steady while stent moves


**Step 2 - Disengaging the Guide:**

- Left hand palm: Pull on the guide to disengage
- Right hand: Simultaneously pull on the stent/wire to prevent diving
- Result: Guide disengages while stent stays in position


### Partial Inflation Stabilization Technique


For additional stability, especially with respiratory motion:

1. When stent is close to optimal position, partially inflate to 2-3 atmospheres
2. This anchors the stent and prevents:      - Diving forward during guide manipulation - Jumping with deep breaths
3. Take time to fine-tune position
4. Can start partial inflation while stent is still partially in guide


**The Absolute Nightmare:**

- Patient takes deep breath while deploying stent
- Stent flies out and ends up partially deployed in the aorta
- **This CANNOT be corrected** - you must live with a stent hanging in the aorta


**If stent dives too distally** = You can add another stent (correctable)  

**If stent flies into aorta** = Catastrophe (not correctable)


## Techniques NOT Recommended


### Floating Wire Technique


- Concept: Advance wire in cusp while guide is disengaged to delineate ostium and prevent guide diving
- **Problems**:     itemize
- Wire may float away from the true ostium
- Gives wrong impression of ostium location
- May cause excessive proximal stenting (into aorta)
- Introduces a fourth element to watch - adds complexity


itemize


### Szabo Technique


- Concept: Floating wire with proximal stent strut hooked to it
- Pre-inflate stent outside body, load wire back-end into distal strut
- **Problems**:     itemize
- Same limitations as floating wire
- Causes significant stent deformation
- In-vitro and IVUS studies show excessive proximal stent protrusion
- Prevents distal miss but **does NOT prevent the ultimate nightmare** (stenting the aorta)


itemize


### Ostial Pro Device


- A short guide extension with feet that appose on aorta
- Prevents guide from diving deep
- **Limitations**:     itemize
- Can still cause excessive proximal stenting
- Introduces fourth element to manage


itemize


**The Key Principle**: In interventional cardiology, stick with the basics and keep things as simple as possible. Don't add unnecessary elements and techniques. Master the fundamental hand maneuvers instead.


## IVUS Verification of Ostial Coverage


**Always verify ostial coverage with IVUS after stenting.**


> On IVUS pullback, you must see at least one frame where:
> itemize[leftmargin=*]
>     \item **One part of the stent** is apposed to the coronary wall (white appearance)
>     \item **Another part of the stent** is in the aorta (same frame)
> itemize
> This confirms the stent covers the ostium properly.
> **Alternative acceptable finding**: One frame where stent is fully surrounded by aorta (if covering both upper and lower edges completely).


If IVUS shows missed ostium, add another stent before closing the case.

## Summary and Key Takeaways


1. **Views**: LAO cranial for LM, steep LAO (40-50°) for RCA - get the cusp as one line with ostium
2. **Never inject while damped/ventricularized** - risk of VF and dissection
3. **Hit-and-run wiring** is the preferred technique for damped ostial lesions; consider iFR wire
4. **Hit-and-run maneuvering** of balloon and stent for PCI with damped guide
5. **Avoid side-hole guides** - false security, masks ischemia, especially dangerous for LM
6. **Avoid JR4 transradially** for ostial RCA - excessive respiratory motion
7. **Focus on the lower edge** - must cover it, don't obsess over upper edge
8. Use **palm of the left hand technique** for precise stent positioning
9. **Partial inflation** (2-3 atm) helps stabilize stent during fine-tuning
10. **Avoid** floating wire, Szabo, and Ostial Pro - add complexity without benefit
11. **Always verify with IVUS** - look for stent in both coronary and aorta in same frame
12. **Rotablation**: Start in guide, burr forward into ostium
13. **Orbital atherectomy**: Engage deep, cut on the way back (never forward through ostium)
