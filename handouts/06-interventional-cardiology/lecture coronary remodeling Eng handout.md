## Introduction


Coronary artery remodeling (vascular remodeling) refers to the adaptive response of the arterial wall to atherosclerotic plaque formation. This concept was first systematically described by pathologist Seymour Glagov in 1987, who discovered that coronary arteries undergo compensatory expansion in the early stages of plaque formation to maintain lumen area.

Understanding coronary artery remodeling is of great importance for clinical diagnosis, risk stratification, and treatment strategy selection. Different types of remodeling patterns are closely related to plaque stability, clinical presentation, and prognosis.


## Definition and Classification of Remodeling


### Basic Definition


Coronary artery remodeling refers to changes in the external elastic membrane (EEM) area in response to the accumulation of atherosclerotic plaques. This process involves reorganization of the vessel wall structure, including extracellular matrix remodeling, smooth muscle cell migration and proliferation, and inflammatory responses.

### Classification Criteria


Based on changes in vessel cross-section, remodeling can be classified into the following types:


| p{11.5cm}}  **Type** | **Definition and Characteristics** |  |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Positive Remodeling | EEM area at the lesion site \textgreater EEM area of the reference segment    Compensatory vessel expansion, relatively maintained lumen area    Remodeling index \textgreater 1.05 |  |
| Negative Remodeling | EEM area at the lesion site \textless EEM area of the reference segment    Vessel constriction, aggravated lumen stenosis    Remodeling index \textless 0.95 |  |
| Intermediate/No Remodeling | Remodeling index between 0.95-1.05    No significant change in vessel area |  |


### Remodeling Index Calculation


The remodeling index is calculated as follows:

\[
Remodeling Index = \text{EEM area at lesion site}{Average EEM area of reference segment}
\]

The reference segment is usually selected from relatively normal vessel segments proximal and distal to the lesion.


## Positive Remodeling


### Pathophysiological Mechanisms


Positive remodeling represents a compensatory expansion response of the vessel. In the early stages of plaque formation, the vessel wall expands outward to accommodate the growing plaque, thereby maintaining lumen area. This process involves multiple mechanisms:


| p{11cm}}  **Mechanism** | **Description** |  |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Matrix Metalloproteinase    (MMPs) Activation | Enzymes such as MMP-2 and MMP-9 degrade extracellular matrix, particularly collagen and elastic fibers, making the vessel wall more prone to expansion |  |
| Inflammatory Cell Infiltration | Inflammatory cells such as macrophages and T lymphocytes release cytokines, promoting vessel expansion |  |
| Smooth Muscle Cell Apoptosis | Apoptosis of medial smooth muscle cells reduces resistance to vessel expansion |  |
| Endothelial Dysfunction | Endothelial cells produce fewer vasoconstrictors and more vasodilators |  |
| Neovascularization | Increased neovascularization in the plaque and adventitia may promote vessel expansion |  |


### Plaque Characteristics


Positive remodeling is typically associated with the following plaque characteristics:


- **Large lipid core**: The plaque center contains abundant lipids, occupying a large proportion of the plaque volume
- **Thin fibrous cap**: The fibrous cap covering the lipid core is thin (\textless 65 \textmu m) and prone to rupture
- **High inflammatory activity**: Abundant macrophage infiltration, elevated inflammatory markers
- **Intraplaque hemorrhage**: Fragile neovessels are prone to rupture and bleeding
- **Less calcification**: Compared to negative remodeling, the degree of calcification is lower


Plaques with positive remodeling are typically "vulnerable plaques" with a higher risk of rupture. These plaques may appear to have mild stenosis on angiography but are actually highly dangerous.


### Clinical Significance


| p{11cm}}  **Clinical Feature** | **Description** |  |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Clinical Presentation | Common in acute coronary syndromes (ACS), particularly acute myocardial infarction (AMI)    Patients may have no significant symptoms or only mild symptoms before plaque rupture |  |
| Angiographic Appearance | Degree of lumen stenosis may not be severe (\textless 50  Lesion danger may be underestimated    Requires IVUS or OCT for confirmation |  |
| Plaque Rupture Risk | High risk: thin fibrous cap, large lipid core, high inflammatory activity    Can lead to acute thrombosis and myocardial infarction |  |
| Prognosis | Associated with higher cardiovascular event risk    Requires aggressive medical therapy and risk factor control |  |


### Imaging Characteristics


**Intravascular Ultrasound (IVUS)**:

- Increased EEM area at lesion site
- Visible hypoechoic lipid core
- Thin or unclear fibrous cap
- Possible intraplaque hemorrhage or calcification


**Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)**:

- High-resolution observation of fibrous cap thickness (\textless 65 \textmu m defined as thin fibrous cap)
- Clear visualization of lipid core extent
- Visible macrophage accumulation
- Assessment of intraplaque microvasculature


**Coronary Computed Tomography Angiography (CCTA)**:

- Napkin-ring sign: ring-shaped enhancement with low-density center
- Spotty calcification: punctate calcification
- Low attenuation plaque: low-density plaque (\textless 30 HU)
- Positive remodeling: remodeling index \textgreater 1.1


## Negative Remodeling


### Pathophysiological Mechanisms


Negative remodeling represents a constrictive response of the vessel. The vessel wall constricts inward, not only failing to compensate for plaque growth but actually aggravating lumen stenosis. The mechanisms of this process include:


| p{11cm}}  **Mechanism** | **Description** |  |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Fibrotic Process | Massive collagen deposition, increased extracellular matrix    Activation of fibroblasts and myofibroblasts    Increased tissue inhibitors of metalloproteinases (TIMPs) |  |
| Smooth Muscle Cell Proliferation | Proliferation and migration of medial smooth muscle cells    Increased contractile phenotype |  |
| Lower Degree of Inflammation | Compared to positive remodeling, milder inflammatory response    Chronic low-grade inflammatory state |  |
| Calcification Deposition | Higher degree of plaque calcification    Increased vessel wall stiffness |  |


### Plaque Characteristics


Negative remodeling is typically associated with the following plaque characteristics:


- **Predominantly fibrous plaque**: Plaque mainly composed of fibrous tissue, relatively small lipid core
- **Thick fibrous cap**: Fibrous cap is thick (\textgreater 65 \textmu m) with more stable structure
- **Heavy calcification**: High degree of calcification within the plaque, possibly dense or sheet-like calcification
- **Low inflammatory activity**: Less macrophage infiltration, lower inflammatory markers
- **Chronic stable process**: Usually represents chronic atherosclerosis


Plaques with negative remodeling are typically "stable plaques" with relatively lower rupture risk, but due to more severe lumen stenosis, they are more likely to cause chronic angina symptoms.


### Clinical Significance


| p{11cm}}  **Clinical Feature** | **Description** |  |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Clinical Presentation | Common in stable angina    Symptoms related to activity, predictable    Gradually declining exercise tolerance    Less frequent acute coronary syndromes |  |
| Angiographic Appearance | Degree of lumen stenosis usually more severe (\textgreater 50-70  Stenosis length may be longer    Angiography can more accurately assess stenosis severity |  |
| Plaque Rupture Risk | Relatively low risk: thick fibrous cap, fibrotic plaque    Plaque more stable, fewer acute thrombotic events |  |
| Prognosis | Associated with chronic symptoms but lower acute event risk    May require revascularization to improve symptoms    PCI success rate usually higher |  |
| Treatment Considerations | Functional ischemia assessment important (FFR, iFR)    Suitable for elective PCI    Better stent expansion, fewer complications |  |


### Imaging Characteristics


**Intravascular Ultrasound (IVUS)**:

- Decreased EEM area at lesion site
- Visible hyperechoic fibrous tissue or calcification
- Thick and clear fibrous cap
- Lipid core smaller or not obvious


**Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)**:

- Thick fibrous cap (\textgreater 65 \textmu m)
- Predominantly fibrous tissue with homogeneous high signal
- Clear borders of calcified plaque
- Less frequent macrophage accumulation


**Coronary Computed Tomography Angiography (CCTA)**:

- Dense calcified plaque
- Obvious lumen stenosis
- Remodeling index \textless 1.0
- Fibrous plaque presents with intermediate density


## Comparison of Two Types of Remodeling


| p{5.5cm}p{5.5cm}}  **Feature** | **Positive Remodeling** | **Negative Remodeling** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Vessel Changes | EEM expansion    RI \textgreater 1.05 | EEM constriction    RI \textless 0.95 |
| Lumen Stenosis | Relatively mild    May be \textless 50 | Relatively severe    Usually \textgreater 50 |
| Plaque Composition | Large lipid core    Thin fibrous cap    Less calcification | Predominantly fibrous plaque    Thick fibrous cap    More calcification |
| Degree of Inflammation | High inflammation    Many macrophages | Low inflammation    Few macrophages |
| Plaque Stability | Vulnerable plaque    High rupture risk | Stable plaque    Low rupture risk |
| Clinical Presentation | Acute coronary syndrome    May have no prodromal symptoms | Stable angina    Activity-related symptoms |
| Acute Event Risk | High risk    Prone to AMI | Low risk    Fewer acute events |
| Angiography | May underestimate danger    IVUS/OCT needed | More accurate assessment of stenosis    Functional assessment important |
| Treatment Strategy | Aggressive medical therapy    Focus on plaque stabilization    Selective PCI | Symptom-oriented treatment    Functional assessment    Suitable for elective PCI |
| Prognosis | Requires close follow-up    High event risk | Relatively stable    Controllable symptoms |


## Clinical Assessment and Imaging Methods


### Coronary Angiography


**Advantages**:

- Gold standard for lumen assessment
- Guide intervention procedures
- Real-time assessment


**Limitations**:

- Lumen-based assessment only
- Cannot assess vessel wall or plaque composition
- May underestimate positive remodeling lesions
- Limited functional assessment


### Intravascular Ultrasound (IVUS)


**Key measurements**:

- EEM area
- Lumen area
- Plaque burden = (EEM area - lumen area) / EEM area
- Remodeling index


**Advantages**:

- Direct visualization of vessel wall
- Accurate measurement of vessel dimensions
- Assessment of plaque composition
- Guide PCI procedures


**Limitations**:

- Invasive procedure
- Limited resolution (\textasciitilde 100-150 \textmu m)
- Difficulty in assessing fibrous cap


### Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)


**Key assessments**:

- Fibrous cap thickness
- Lipid core extent
- Macrophage infiltration
- Thrombus detection
- Neovascularization


**Advantages**:

- Highest resolution (10-20 \textmu m)
- Excellent fibrous cap visualization
- Detailed plaque characterization
- Stent optimization


**Limitations**:

- Invasive procedure
- Limited penetration depth
- Requires blood clearance
- Cannot accurately measure EEM


### Coronary CT Angiography (CCTA)


**High-risk plaque features**:

- Positive remodeling (RI \textgreater 1.1)
- Low attenuation plaque
- Napkin-ring sign
- Spotty calcification


**Advantages**:

- Non-invasive
- Comprehensive assessment of coronary tree
- Assessment of extravascular structures
- Ideal tool for long-term follow-up


### Integrated Assessment Strategy


| p{11cm}}  **Clinical Scenario** | **Recommended Assessment Strategy** |  |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Acute Coronary Syndrome | Coronary angiography as primary    Consider IVUS/OCT assessment of culprit lesion    Assess remodeling pattern to guide treatment |  |
| Stable Angina | CCTA for initial assessment    Functional assessment (FFR-CT, exercise testing)    Invasive assessment + IVUS if necessary |  |
| Asymptomatic High-risk | CCTA screening    Assess high-risk plaque features    Aggressive risk factor control |  |
| Pre-PCI Assessment | IVUS/OCT assessment of lesion    Measure vessel size    Assess degree of calcification    Guide stent selection |  |


## Treatment Strategies


### Treatment of Positive Remodeling


**Medical therapy** (focus on plaque stabilization):

- **High-intensity statin**: Atorvastatin 40-80 mg or Rosuvastatin 20-40 mg
- **Antiplatelet agents**: Aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor (such as Ticagrelor or Prasugrel)
- **PCSK9 inhibitors**: Consider for very high-risk patients (LDL-C target \textless 55 mg/dL)
- **Blood pressure control**: ACEI or ARB
- **Glycemic control**: Aggressive control in diabetic patients (GLP-1 RA or SGLT2i preferred)
- **Lifestyle**: Smoking cessation, dietary control, regular exercise


**Interventional treatment considerations**:

- Emergency PCI needed in acute coronary syndrome
- Consider drug-eluting stents (DES)
- Assess need for imaging guidance (IVUS/OCT-guided PCI)
- Attention to prevention of no-reflow phenomenon
- Consider using thrombus aspiration or distal protection devices


For vulnerable plaques with positive remodeling, even if lumen stenosis is not severe, if high-risk features are present (large lipid core, thin fibrous cap, high inflammatory activity), aggressive medical therapy should still be pursued to stabilize the plaque and prevent acute events.


### Treatment of Negative Remodeling


**Medical therapy** (focus on symptom control):

- **Anti-anginal medications**: Beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, long-acting nitrates
- **Statin therapy**: Moderate to high intensity
- **Antiplatelet agents**: Usually aspirin alone is sufficient
- **Risk factor control**: Blood pressure, glucose, lipids


**Interventional treatment considerations**:

- Symptom-guided elective PCI
- Functional assessment (FFR \textless 0.80 or iFR \textless 0.89)
- Calcified lesions may require special handling:     itemize
- Rotational atherectomy
- Cutting balloon
- Intravascular lithotripsy (IVL)


    \item Stent expansion usually better
    \item Relatively lower complication risk

### Long-term Follow-up Strategy


| p{11cm}}  **Remodeling Type** | **Follow-up Recommendations** |  |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Positive Remodeling | Intensive follow-up (3-6 months)    Strict LDL-C control (target \textless 55 mg/dL)    Consider CCTA to assess plaque progression    Evaluate high-risk plaques in other vessels    Continue dual antiplatelet therapy (extend if indicated) |  |
| Negative Remodeling | Routine follow-up (6-12 months)    Focus on symptom assessment    Functional assessment if necessary    Optimize medical therapy    Exercise rehabilitation program |  |


## Clinical Case Analysis


### Case 1: Acute Myocardial Infarction Due to Positive Remodeling


**History**: 52-year-old male with 30-year smoking history, mild hyperlipidemia, no diabetes. Usually no significant symptoms, sudden chest pain and cold sweats upon waking one morning.

**Examination findings**:

- ECG: ST elevation in leads V2-V4
- Troponin I: significantly elevated (25 ng/mL)
- Emergency coronary angiography: 50
- IVUS findings: obvious positive remodeling (RI = 1.3), large lipid core, ruptured thin fibrous cap, thrombus formation


**Treatment**:

- Emergency PCI with DES
- Dual antiplatelet therapy (Aspirin + Ticagrelor)
- High-intensity statin (Atorvastatin 80 mg)
- Subsequently added PCSK9 inhibitor
- Smoking cessation counseling


**Learning points**:

- Lesions with positive remodeling may appear to have mild stenosis on angiography
- Vulnerable plaques can rupture suddenly without obvious prodromal symptoms
- IVUS helps with correct diagnosis and treatment decisions
- Aggressive medical therapy needed to prevent recurrence


### Case 2: Stable Angina with Negative Remodeling


**History**: 68-year-old female with 10-year history of diabetes and hypertension. Chest tightness during exercise for the past six months, relieved by rest. Symptoms gradually worsening.

**Examination findings**:

- Exercise ECG: positive (ST depression in leads II, III, aVF)
- CCTA: 70
- Coronary angiography confirmation: RCA 75
- FFR measurement: 0.75 (functionally significant)


**Treatment**:

- Rotational atherectomy-assisted PCI
- DES implantation
- Long-term aspirin use
- Statin, ACEI, beta-blocker
- Optimized diabetes control


**Learning points**:

- Negative remodeling typically presents as stable angina
- Functional assessment helps determine need for PCI
- Calcified lesions may require special techniques
- Prognosis relatively good, symptoms can be effectively controlled


## Latest Research Advances


### Molecular Mechanism Research


Recent years have brought deeper understanding of the molecular mechanisms of remodeling:

**Factors related to Positive Remodeling**:

- **MMPs/TIMPs imbalance**: Increased expression of MMP-2 and MMP-9, decreased TIMP-1 and TIMP-2
- **Inflammatory factors**: Elevated IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α
- **Oxidative stress**: Increased ROS generation, decreased antioxidant capacity
- **Angiogenesis**: Increased VEGF expression, formation of fragile neovessels


**Factors related to Negative Remodeling**:

- **TGF-β pathway**: Promotes fibrosis and collagen deposition
- **PDGF**: Promotes smooth muscle cell proliferation and migration
- **Bone morphogenetic proteins**: Promote vascular calcification


### Biomarkers


Research has found certain biomarkers are associated with remodeling types:


| p{5cm}p{5.5cm}}  **Marker** | **Positive Remodeling** | **Negative Remodeling** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| High-sensitivity C-reactive protein | Significantly elevated | Mildly elevated or normal |
| MMP-9 | Elevated | Normal or decreased |
| TIMP-1 | Decreased | Elevated |
| Osteopontin | Elevated | Elevated (but different mechanism) |
| Troponin | May be elevated (minor injury) | Usually normal |


### New Imaging Technologies


**Near-Infrared Spectroscopy (NIRS)**:

- Detects lipid core
- Can be combined with IVUS (NIRS-IVUS)
- Predicts high-risk plaques


**Intravascular Photoacoustic Imaging (IVPA)**:

- Provides both structural and compositional information
- Detects lipids and hemoglobin
- Still in development stage


**Artificial Intelligence Applications**:

- Automated analysis of CCTA images
- Predict plaque progression
- Risk stratification


## Summary and Clinical Recommendations


importantbox
**Key Points**


1. **Positive remodeling** represents compensatory vessel expansion, often accompanied by vulnerable plaques with high acute event risk
2. **Negative remodeling** represents vessel constriction, usually stable fibrotic plaques with more obvious symptoms but lower acute risk
3. Angiography may underestimate the danger of positive remodeling; intravascular imaging (IVUS/OCT) helps accurate assessment
4. Treatment strategies should be individualized based on remodeling type: positive remodeling emphasizes plaque stabilization, negative remodeling emphasizes symptom control
5. Long-term follow-up and strict risk factor control are important for both types

### Clinical Practice Recommendations


| p{10.5cm}}  **Scenario** | **Recommendations** |  |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Acute Coronary Syndrome | Consider IVUS assessment of culprit lesion remodeling pattern    Assess high-risk features of other non-culprit lesions    Aggressive medical therapy to stabilize all vulnerable plaques |  |
| Moderate Stenosis Lesions | Consider intravascular imaging to assess for positive remodeling    Do not underestimate danger based on angiography alone    Positive remodeling with high-risk features should be treated aggressively |  |
| Heavily Calcified Lesions | Usually indicates negative remodeling    Assess functional significance (FFR/iFR)    Prepare appropriate calcification management strategy |  |
| Asymptomatic High-risk Patients | CCTA screening for high-risk plaque features    Assess positive remodeling and other high-risk features    Aggressive primary prevention |  |


### Future Prospects


- **Precision medicine**: Individualized risk assessment based on genetics, biomarkers, and imaging
- **Drug development**: New drugs targeting specific remodeling mechanisms
- **Imaging technology**: Higher resolution, non-invasive plaque assessment tools
- **Artificial intelligence**: Automated plaque analysis and risk prediction
- **Prevention strategies**: Early identification and intervention of high-risk plaques
