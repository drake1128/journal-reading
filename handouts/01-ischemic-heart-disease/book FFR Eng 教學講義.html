1 Fractional Flow Reserve (FFR): Comprehensive Lecture Notes

整理：謝慕揚 MD, PhD, FESC 日期：2025-11-29

1.1 1. Theoretical Foundation

1.1.1 1.1 FFR Definition and Principles

Fractional Flow Reserve (FFR) is a physiological index used to assess the hemodynamic significance of coronary artery stenosis.

1.1.1.1 1.1.1 Basic Definition

FFR = Pd / Pa

Where: - Pd = Distal coronary pressure (pressure distal to stenosis) - Pa = Aortic pressure (proximal pressure)

1.1.1.2 1.1.2 Theoretical Derivation

FFR represents the ratio of maximum achievable blood flow in the presence of stenosis to the theoretical maximum flow without stenosis in the myocardial territory supplied by that coronary artery.

According to Poiseuille’s law, blood flow is proportional to the pressure gradient: Q = ΔP / R

Under maximal hyperemia (induced by adenosine):

FFR = Qmax,stenosis / Qmax,normal = (Pd - Pv) / (Pa - Pv) ≈ Pd / Pa

When Pv (central venous pressure) is much smaller than Pa and Pd, this simplifies to FFR = Pd/Pa.

1.1.1.3 1.1.3 Clinical Significance

FFR Value Clinical Significance
FFR > 0.80 No hemodynamic significance, PCI not recommended
FFR ≤ 0.80 Hemodynamically significant, PCI recommended
FFR < 0.75 Definite ischemia, strong recommendation for intervention
0.75 < FFR < 0.80 Gray zone, requires clinical judgment
FFR < 0.50 Severe ischemia, high-risk lesion

1.2 2. Technical Aspects

1.2.1 2.1 Measurement Technique

1.2.1.1 2.1.1 Equipment Requirements

  • Pressure wire
  • Hyperemic agents: Adenosine (140-180 µg/kg/min IV) or IC bolus (40-80 µg)
  • Pressure measurement system
  • Angiography equipment

1.2.1.2 2.1.2 Measurement Protocol

Step Operational Details
Calibration Zero the pressure wire and measurement system
Equalization Confirm Pd = Pa at guide catheter position
Wire advancement Advance pressure wire distal to lesion
Induce hyperemia Administer adenosine for maximal hyperemia
Record pressure Record Pd and Pa during hyperemia
Calculate FFR Automatic or manual calculation of FFR = Pd/Pa
Pullback verification Withdraw wire to guide catheter to check for drift

1.2.2 2.2 Non-Hyperemic Indices

1.2.2.1 2.2.1 iFR (Instantaneous Wave-free Ratio)

iFR is measured during a specific period of the cardiac cycle (wave-free period) without hyperemic agents:

  • Measurement timing: Mid-diastole (approximately 25% of cardiac cycle)
  • Cutoff: iFR ≤ 0.89 considered significant
  • Advantages: No adenosine required, shorter procedure time, fewer side effects
  • Correlation with FFR: Approximately 80-85% concordance

1.3 3. Wheatstone Bridge Principle

1.3.1 3.1 Application to Pressure Measurement

Pressure wire systems utilize the Wheatstone Bridge principle to convert mechanical pressure into electrical signals.

1.3.1.1 3.1.1 Basic Bridge Structure

The bridge consists of four resistors, with at least one being a pressure-sensitive resistor (piezoresistive sensor):

Vout = Vin × (R3/(R3+R4) - R2/(R1+R2))

When pressure changes, the resistance of the pressure-sensitive element changes, causing a voltage output variation that allows pressure measurement.

1.3.1.2 3.1.2 Application in FFR Systems

Component Function
Pressure sensor Located at wire tip, contains piezoresistive elements
Signal conversion Converts pressure changes to voltage changes
Amplifier Amplifies minute voltage signals
Calibration system Eliminates temperature, drift, and other interference
Digital conversion Converts analog signals to digital pressure readings

1.3.2 3.2 Technical Considerations

  • Drift: Prolonged measurements may cause signal drift; calibration before and after is essential
  • Temperature compensation: Body temperature variations can affect resistance values
  • Sensitivity: Modern pressure wires can detect pressure changes within ±2 mmHg

1.4 4. FAME 1 & FAME 2 Trials

1.4.1 4.1 FAME 1 (2009)

Category Details
Full Name Fractional Flow Reserve versus Angiography for Multivessel Evaluation
Publication Year 2009
Journal New England Journal of Medicine
Study Design Multicenter RCT
Sample Size 1,005 patients
Inclusion Criteria Multivessel coronary artery disease suitable for PCI
Exclusion Criteria STEMI <5 days, history of CABG
Intervention FFR arm: Treat only lesions with FFR ≤0.80; Angio arm: Treat all lesions with diameter stenosis ≥50%
Primary Endpoint 1-year MACE (death, MI, repeat revascularization)
Primary Results FFR arm MACE: 13.2% vs Angio arm 18.3% (P=0.02); HR: 0.72 (95% CI: 0.54-0.96)
Secondary Results FFR arm used fewer stents (1.9 vs 2.7, P<0.001); Similar procedure time; Similar contrast volume
5-Year Follow-up MACE: 28% (FFR) vs 31% (Angio), P=0.31; FFR arm significantly better in first 2 years, similar from 2-5 years

1.4.2 4.2 FAME 2 (2012-2018)

Category Details
Full Name Fractional Flow Reserve-Guided PCI versus Medical Therapy in Stable Coronary Disease
Publication Year 2012 (initial), 2018 (5-year)
Journal New England Journal of Medicine
Study Design Multicenter RCT
Sample Size 1,220 patients with FFR ≤0.80
Inclusion Criteria Stable CAD with at least one stenosis and FFR ≤0.80
Randomization PCI + OMT arm: 888 patients; OMT only arm: 332 patients; Registry (FFR >0.80): 166 patients
Primary Endpoint (2-year) Death, MI, or urgent revascularization: PCI + OMT 4.3% vs OMT only 12.7%; HR: 0.32 (95% CI: 0.19-0.53), P<0.001
Components Death: 0.9% vs 1.5% (P=0.49); MI: 3.4% vs 3.4% (P=0.98); Urgent revascularization: 1.6% vs 11.1% (P<0.001)
5-Year Results Primary endpoint: 13.9% vs 27.0%; HR: 0.46 (95% CI: 0.34-0.63), P<0.001; Death or MI: 8.1% vs 12.0%; HR: 0.66 (95% CI: 0.43-1.00), P=0.05
Registry Patients FFR >0.80 with medical therapy only; 5-year event rate: 9.0%; Lower than both randomized arms
Key Message FFR-guided PCI significantly reduces urgent revascularization; Trend toward reduced death/MI at 5 years; Validates FFR ≤0.80 as intervention threshold

1.5 5. Complete Revascularization in ACS

1.5.1 5.1 Landmark Trials in STEMI with Multivessel Disease

Trial Journal/Year N Primary Findings
PRAMI NEJM 2013 — Preventive PCI reduced composite endpoint; HR 0.35 (0.21-0.58), P<0.001; First to show benefit
CvLPRIT JACC 2015 — Complete revasc reduced MACE; HR 0.45 (0.24-0.84), P=0.009; Confirmed PRAMI findings
COMPLETE NEJM 2019 4,041 Largest trial; Primary EP: CV death/MI; HR 0.74 (0.60-0.91), P=0.004; CV death/MI: 7.8% vs 10.5% at 3 years; Guideline-changing evidence
MULTISTARS AMI NEJM 2023 — Immediate vs Staged complete revasc; Primary EP: 8.5% vs 7.3%; HR 1.15 (0.73-1.82); Non-inferiority proven
FULL REVASC NEJM 2024 1,542 FFR-guided complete vs Culprit-only; 4.8-year follow-up; Primary EP: 19.0% vs 20.4%; HR 0.93 (0.74-1.16), P=0.51; Neutral result
FIRE NEJM 2023 1,445 Physiology-guided complete vs Culprit-only (≥75 years); CV death/MI/revasc: 23.3% vs 30.0%; HR 0.73 (0.58-0.90), P=0.004; Benefit in elderly

1.5.2 5.2 Timing of Complete Revascularization

Trial Journal/Year N Key Findings
COMPLETE Timing NEJM 2019 4,041 In-hospital staged vs Post-discharge staged; Similar outcomes, both superior to culprit-only
MULTISTARS AMI NEJM 2023 — Immediate vs Staged (19-45 days); Non-inferiority proven; Staged approach acceptable
SMILE JACC 2016 — Single-stage vs Multi-stage (in-hospital); Single-stage superior; HR 0.55 (0.36-0.83), P=0.004

1.5.3 5.3 Cardiogenic Shock Special Considerations

Trial Journal/Year N Primary Results and Clinical Implications
CULPRIT-SHOCK NEJM 2017 — 30 days: Death/RRT: 45.9% (culprit-only) vs 55.4% (MV-PCI); RR 0.83 (0.71-0.96), P=0.01; Mortality: 43.3% vs 51.6%, P=0.03. 1 year: Mortality: 50.0% vs 56.9% (P=0.07); HF rehospitalization: 4.5% vs 0.9% (P=0.01). Landmark trial: Changed guideline recommendations; Culprit-only preferred in cardiogenic shock; Consider staged revascularization

1.5.4 5.4 FFR-guided vs Angiography-guided Complete Revascularization

Trial Journal/Year N Main Results
DANAMI-3 PRIMULTI Lancet 2015 — FFR-guided reduced PCI need; Approximately 50% lesions FFR >0.80; MACE improved
COMPARE-ACUTE NEJM 2017 — FFR-guided immediate complete; Primary EP: HR 0.35 (P<0.001); Supports FFR-guided strategy
FLOWER-MI NEJM 2021 1,171 FFR non-superior to angio; Primary EP: 5.5% vs 4.2%; HR 1.32 (0.78-2.23), P=0.31; Stent use: 1.01 vs 1.50
FRAME-AMI European Heart J — FFR-guided superior to angio
FULL REVASC NEJM 2024 1,542 FFR-guided vs culprit-only; 4.8-year follow-up: neutral result; HR 0.93 (0.74-1.16), P=0.51

1.5.5 5.5 Meta-Analyses and Systematic Reviews

Meta-analysis Journal/Year Main Conclusions
Elgendy et al. JACC Cardiovasc Interv 2017 8 RCTs, 7,030 patients; Complete revasc significantly reduced CV death and MI; OR 0.74 (0.61-0.89)
Pavasini et al. Eur Heart J 2020 9 RCTs; Complete revasc reduced CV death; HR 0.72 (0.58-0.89)
Biscaglia et al. PLOS ONE 2024 8 RCTs, 4,433 patients; FFR vs angio-guided analysis; MACE: RR 0.69 (0.52-0.90); ACS subgroup: RR 0.77 (0.51-1.17)

1.6 6. Clinical Practice Guidelines Summary

1.6.1 6.1 Current Guideline Recommendations

Clinical Scenario Recommendations
STEMI + MVD (no shock) Class I: Complete revascularization recommended; Can be immediate or staged (in-hospital or post-discharge); Consider FFR/iFR guidance for non-culprit lesions
STEMI + MVD + Shock Class I: Culprit-only PCI; Class IIa: Consider staged revascularization; Based on CULPRIT-SHOCK trial
NSTEMI + MVD Class IIa: Complete revascularization; Staged approach more common than immediate; Can use FFR/iFR to assess non-culprit lesions
Elderly Patients (≥75 years) Based on FIRE trial; Physiology-guided complete revasc beneficial; Assess surgical risk and benefit
Presence of CTO STEMI stable patients: Immediate CTO PCI not recommended; Cardiogenic shock: CTO should not be treated immediately; Consider subsequent staged intervention

1.6.2 6.2 FFR/iFR Usage Recommendations

Scenario Recommendations and Evidence Level
Stable CAD multivessel disease Class I, Level A: FFR ≤0.80 recommend PCI; FFR >0.80 can safely defer
STEMI non-culprit lesions Class IIa, Level B: Can use FFR/iFR for assessment; Helps decide immediate or staged PCI
NSTEMI non-culprit lesions Class IIa, Level B: FFR/iFR helps reduce unnecessary PCI; Safe and effective compared to angio-guided
Timing of measurement (ACS) Index procedure or staged (days later) both acceptable; iFR may be more suitable in acute phase (no adenosine needed)
Not recommended Culprit lesion (clearly needs immediate PCI); Cardiogenic shock (CULPRIT-SHOCK); STEMI with left main disease

1.7 7. Controversies and Future Directions

1.7.1 7.1 Current Controversies

Controversy Discussion
FLOWER-MI neutral results FFR-guided failed to prove superiority over angio-guided; Possible reasons: modern angio assessment already accurate, low event rate, trial design; Does not mean FFR is useless; both methods acceptable
Timing of complete revasc Immediate vs staged still debated; MULTISTARS AMI showed non-inferiority; Immediate may increase contrast volume, procedural complexity; Staged requires rehospitalization
FFR accuracy in ACS Microvascular dysfunction may affect accuracy; Most studies show good reliability; iFR may be alternative option
FAME 2 soft endpoint issue Main benefit from reduced urgent revascularization; No significant difference in death/MI (trend at 5 years); Non-blinded design may introduce bias
Cost-effectiveness FFR equipment cost vs reduced unnecessary PCI; Long-term follow-up shows cost-effective; Requires additional training and equipment

1.7.2 7.2 Future Research Directions

  • Integration of imaging and physiology: Combined IVUS/OCT + FFR assessment
  • Novel physiologic indices: dPR, RFR, virtual FFR (computed FFR)
  • Artificial intelligence applications: AI-assisted FFR interpretation, outcome prediction
  • Special populations: Optimal strategies for diabetes, chronic kidney disease, very elderly patients
  • Long-term outcomes: >5-year follow-up of complete revascularization effects
  • Precision medicine: Gene and biomarker-guided personalized treatment

1.8 8. Conclusions

FFR represents a major advancement in percutaneous coronary intervention, providing an objective physiological assessment standard. Key conclusions include:

  1. Stable CAD: FFR-guided PCI superior to pure angiography-guided approach (FAME 1 & 2)
  2. STEMI with multivessel disease: Complete revascularization superior to culprit-only (COMPLETE and others)
  3. FFR in ACS: Safe to use for non-culprit lesion assessment
  4. Cardiogenic shock: Culprit-only is preferred strategy (CULPRIT-SHOCK)
  5. Timing choice: Both immediate and staged complete revascularization are acceptable
  6. Elderly patients: Physiology-guided approach still beneficial (FIRE)

The application of FFR and iFR should be integrated with clinical judgment, imaging assessment, and patient characteristics to achieve optimal personalized treatment strategies.
