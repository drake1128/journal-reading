## Overview


The STRIVE trial is a randomized controlled trial evaluating the use of low-dose intracoronary recombinant tissue plasminogen activator (alteplase/TPA) in patients with ST-segment elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI) and large thrombus burden undergoing primary percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI). The study was presented as a late-breaker at the TCT 2025 conference on October 28, 2025, and simultaneously published in the Journal of the American College of Cardiology (JACC). This summary is based on the [JACC podcast](https://www.jacc.org/digital-content/podcast/alteplase-adjunct-primary-pci-jacc-tct-2025) interview, featuring Dr. Yusuf Ahmad from UCSF interviewing the principal investigator, Dr. Shamir Mehta.

## Background and Rationale


### Core Problem: Microvascular Obstruction


****Microvascular Obstruction (MVO) is the most important and still unresolved problem in STEMI patients undergoing primary PCI.****

**Clinical Significance:**

- Approximately **50
- Leads to coronary microvascular obstruction
- Results in poor myocardial tissue perfusion
- Consequences:     itemize
- Larger infarct size
- Higher incidence of heart failure
- Higher incidence of cardiogenic shock
- Higher mortality

### STRIVE's Innovative Strategy: Targeted Drug Delivery


**Theoretical Basis:**

1. **Thrombolytic Mechanism:** Dissolving thrombi is the fundamental mechanism of thrombolytics
2. **Pathophysiological Targeting:** Microvascular obstruction is caused by distal thrombus embolization
3. **Direct Delivery:** Direct delivery of drug to microcirculation via microcatheter
4. **Dose Optimization:** Using significantly reduced doses (only 10-20
5. **Concentration Advantage:** Achieving 10-20 times higher concentration in coronary microcirculation


**Comparison with Systemic Administration:**

| p{8cm}}  **Systemic IV Administration** | **Intracoronary Administration (STRIVE)** |  |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Systemic IV Administration** | **Intracoronary Administration (STRIVE)** |  |
| Standard dose: 100mg alteplase | Low dose: 10mg or 20mg |  |
| Hepatic first-pass effect | Direct targeting to lesion |  |
| Systemic distribution, diluted concentration | High microcirculation concentration (10-20x) |  |
| Higher bleeding risk | Theoretically lower bleeding risk |  |
| Failed to improve outcomes | To be validated by STRIVE |  |

## Study Methods


### Trial Design


**Study Type:** Prospective, 3-arm parallel, double-blind, placebo-controlled randomized trial

**Design Rationale:**

- First demonstrate improvement in microvascular obstruction before conducting a large-scale outcome trial
- Analogy: Like antihypertensive drugs must first prove they lower blood pressure
- If unable to improve the basic pathophysiological mechanism, unlikely to improve clinical outcomes


### Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria


**Inclusion Criteria - Enrichment Strategy for High-Risk Patients:**


1. **Large STEMI:**      - **Large anterior MI**, or - **Large inferior MI:** Total ST-segment deviation at least 8 millimeters
2. **Large Thrombus Burden:**      - Angiographically visible large thrombus - 95


**Exclusion Criteria:**

- Small STEMI
- Low thrombus burden


**Importance of Enrichment Strategy:**
quote
"We wanted to prove this strategy works, and we need to include patients with large thrombus."  

--- Shamir Mehta, MD
quote


### Intervention


**Randomization (1:1:1):**

1. **Low-dose group:** Intracoronary alteplase 10mg (n=69)
2. **High-dose group:** Intracoronary alteplase 20mg (n=70)
3. **Control group:** Normal saline (matched placebo) (n=70)


**Total Enrollment:** 209 patients

**Administration Procedure:**

1. Establish myocardial reperfusion (usually with thrombus aspiration or balloon catheter)
2. Achieve TIMI flow grade
3. Position microcatheter distally in coronary artery
4. Inject study drug over approximately **3 minutes**
5. Proceed with stent implantation


### Study Endpoints


**Primary Endpoint:**

- **Post-procedure Myocardial Blush Grade (MBG)**     itemize
- Incidence of MBG 0/1
- Or distal embolization


itemize

**Secondary Endpoints:**

- **30-minute ST-segment resolution**
- **TIMI flow grade**
- **TIMI myocardial perfusion grade**
- Other microvascular perfusion parameters


**Safety Endpoints:**

- Major bleeding events
- Clinically relevant non-major bleeding
- Arrhythmias (especially ventricular fibrillation)

## Study Interpretation


### Why Did the Strategy Fail?


**Dr. Mehta's Insights:**


1. **Wrong target hypothesis:**     quote     "We may not be looking at microvascular *obstruction*, we may be looking at microvascular *obliteration*."     quote      - The problem may not be just blockage - It may be irreversible destruction - Structural damage to microvascular bed
2. **Timing issues:**      - Damage may already be done before PCI - Window for intervention may be too narrow - Reperfusion injury mechanisms
3. **Mechanism limitations:**      - Thrombolysis alone insufficient - Multiple pathophysiological mechanisms involved:         itemize - Myocardial hemorrhage - Myocardial edema - Inflammation - Endothelial dysfunction       itemize


### Comparison with Previous Studies


**Why STRIVE differs from earlier positive studies:**


1. **Higher quality evidence:**      - Double-blind design - Placebo-controlled - Adequate sample size for primary endpoint - Rigorous methodology
2. **Contemporary practice:**      - Modern stent technology - Potent antiplatelet therapy - Optimized reperfusion strategies - Better baseline outcomes
3. **Different patient population:**      - Enriched for high-risk features - Large thrombus burden - Large infarct size - If it works anywhere, should work here


## Clinical Implications


### For Current Practice


**Clear recommendations:**


1. ****Do NOT use intracoronary low-dose TPA routinely in STEMI****
2. ****No benefit demonstrated even in highest-risk patients****
3. ****Potential safety signal regarding ventricular fibrillation****
4. Continue using evidence-based strategies:      - Rapid reperfusion - Optimal antithrombotic therapy - Selective thrombus aspiration in appropriate cases


### For Future Research


**Paradigm shift needed:**


1. **Rethink microvascular obstruction:**      - Is it obstruction or obliteration? - Is it reversible or irreversible? - What is the therapeutic window?
2. **New therapeutic targets:**      - Endothelial protection - Anti-inflammatory strategies - Reduction of reperfusion injury - Promotion of microvascular repair
3. **Novel approaches:**      - Cell-based therapies - Gene therapy - Nanotechnology - Targeted drug delivery systems

## Study Limitations


### Trial Design-Related Limitations


1. **Sample Size:**      - Medium-sized trial with 209 patients - Powered for microvascular obstruction as primary endpoint - Not designed to evaluate clinical outcomes
2. **Endpoint Selection:**      - Primary endpoint is a surrogate endpoint - Myocardial Blush Grade is subjective - Long-term clinical outcomes not evaluated
3. **Timing of Administration:**      - Drug administered before stenting - Post-stent administration might yield different results (Costa's suggestion) - Different timing strategies not explored


### Drug and Dosage-Related Limitations


1. **Drug Selection:**      - Only tested alteplase (TPA) - Other thrombolytics might have different results - Different plasminogen activators not compared
2. **Dose Range:**      - Tested 10mg and 20mg - Higher or lower doses not explored - Both tested doses ineffective

## Conclusions and Future Directions


### Key Messages from STRIVE Trial


1. ****Definitively Negative:**** Low-dose intracoronary TPA does not improve microvascular obstruction in STEMI patients with large thrombus burden
2. ****Safety Concern:**** Possible increased risk of ventricular fibrillation (though not statistically significant)
3. ****Not Recommended:**** This strategy should not be used routinely in clinical practice
4. ****High-Quality Evidence:**** Well-designed double-blind randomized controlled trial with credible results
5. ****Enrichment Strategy:**** Ineffective even in highest-risk patients (large STEMI + large thrombus)


### Future of Microvascular Obstruction Research


**Paradigm Shift Needed:**
quote
*"We may not be looking at microvascular obstruction, we may be looking at microvascular obliteration."*  

--- Shamir Mehta, MD
quote

**Key Questions:**

- Is it still just an "obstruction" problem?
- Or is it irreversible "obliteration"?
- Need completely different therapeutic approach?


**New Research Focus:**

1. **Deep mechanistic research:**      - Role of myocardial hemorrhage - Mechanisms of myocardial edema - Microvascular structural destruction - Time window for irreversible injury
2. **Novel therapeutic strategies:**      - Beyond thrombus removal - Microvascular endothelial protection - Reduction of reperfusion injury - Modulation of inflammatory response - Promotion of microvascular repair


### Recommendations for Clinicians


**Current Best Practices:**

1. **Timely reperfusion:** Rapid restoration of blood flow is key
2. **Optimal antithrombotic therapy:**      - Potent P2Y12 inhibitors - Appropriate dose of heparin - Consider GP IIb/IIIa inhibitors for large thrombus
3. **Selective thrombus aspiration:**      - Consider for large thrombus - Use newer generation devices - Not routine use
4. **Appropriate stent strategy:**      - Use intravascular imaging for assessment - 1:1 sizing - Avoid unnecessary post-dilation
5. **Continuous learning:**      - Follow new research developments - Participate in clinical trials - Remain open to new devices and strategies


**Ongoing Challenge:**
quote
*"Microvascular obstruction continues to be a problem."*  

--- Shamir Mehta, MD
quote

Despite the negative results from the STRIVE trial, it provides important insights into the complexity of microvascular obstruction and points out that future research needs to more deeply explore fundamental pathophysiological mechanisms to find truly effective treatment strategies.
