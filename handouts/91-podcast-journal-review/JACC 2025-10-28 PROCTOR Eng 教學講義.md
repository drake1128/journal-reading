## Overview


The PROCTOR trial (Percutaneous Coronary Intervention of Native Coronary Artery versus Saphenous Vein Graft in Patients with Prior Coronary Artery Bypass Graft Surgery) represents the first randomized study comparing clinical outcomes between a strategy of native vessel PCI and saphenous vein graft (SVG) PCI in post-CABG patients. The study was presented as a late-breaker at the TCT 2025 conference on October 28, 2025, with simultaneous publication in the Journal of the American College of Cardiology (JACC). This summary is based on the [JACC video interview](https://www.jacc.org/digital-content/video/proctor-trial-jacc-tct-2025) featuring JACC Associate Editor Yousif Ahmad interviewing first author Ruben W. de Winter.

## Background and Rationale


### Core Problem: Revascularization After CABG


****Long-term management of post-CABG patients faces critical challenges: saphenous vein graft failure and progression of native coronary artery disease.****

**Clinical Significance:**

- Despite advances in secondary prevention and surgical techniques, long-term efficacy of CABG remains limited
- Up to **60
- Many patients require return to the catheterization laboratory for repeat revascularization
- Repeat revascularization represents a complex and high-risk clinical decision

### Limitations of Current Guidelines


**Current Guideline Recommendations:**

- Prioritize native coronary artery PCI over bypass graft PCI
- Recommendation Class: **IIa**
- Level of Evidence: **B**
- ****Major Limitation: This recommendation is based entirely on observational data, lacking randomized controlled trial evidence****


**Clinical Practice Dilemma:**

1. Native vessel PCI is technically challenging but potentially more durable
2. SVG PCI is simpler but with uncertain long-term outcomes
3. Lack of high-quality evidence to guide clinical decisions
4. Difficulty in individualized assessment


## Study Methods


### Trial Design


**Study Type:** Investigator-initiated, open-label randomized controlled trial

**Design Features:**

- **Strategy Trial:** Comparing two revascularization strategies
- **Multicenter:** 14 centers across Europe
- **Randomization:** 1:1 to native vessel PCI or SVG PCI strategy
- **Using interactive web-based randomization platform**


**Design Philosophy:**
quote
"We call it a strategy trial because the heart team first defines the indication for percutaneous revascularization, then we compare two treatment strategies."  

--- Ruben W. de Winter, MD
quote


### Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria


**Inclusion Criteria:**


1. **SVG Stenosis:**      - SVG diameter stenosis >50 - **Excluded totally occluded SVG (Class III recommendation)**
2. **Revascularization Indication:**      - Clear indication for percutaneous revascularization as defined by heart team
3. **Technical Feasibility (Critical Criterion):**      - Both native coronary artery and SVG lesions **must** be prospectively deemed technically feasible for PCI by the heart team - Ensures fair randomization - Increased enrollment difficulty


**SVG Group Exclusion Criteria:**

- Occlusive disease
- Early graft failure
- High-risk graft features: aneurysm or heavy thrombus formation


**Native Vessel Group:**

- No exclusion criteria
- All native vessel lesions eligible for inclusion


**Important Time Parameter:**

- Mean interval from CABG to PROCTOR trial index PCI: **18 years**
- This extended period results in:     itemize
- Extensive disease in native vessels
- Extensive disease in bypass grafts
- Some grafts may have undergone prior interventions


itemize


### Study Hypothesis


Based on observational studies, the hypothesis was:

- Native vessel PCI group would have better outcomes
- Lower MACE rates
- Better long-term prognosis


### Study Endpoints


**Primary Endpoint:**

- **1-year Major Adverse Cardiovascular Events (MACE)**     itemize
- All-cause mortality
- Myocardial infarction
- Clinically driven target vessel revascularization


itemize

**Secondary Endpoints:**

- PCI-related myocardial infarction
- Clinically driven target coronary territory revascularization
- Individual MACE components
- Procedure-related complications

### Results Interpretation


**Short-term Advantage (1 year):**

1. SVG PCI demonstrated early clinical advantage
2. Primarily due to high procedural complexity of native vessel PCI
3. More PCI-related complications (especially myocardial infarction)
4. Higher need for target territory revascularization


**Long-term Concerns:**

1. SVG disease tends to progress more rapidly
2. Restenosis and reocclusion rates may not be fully apparent at 1-year follow-up
3. **Results at 3-5 years may be completely different**
4. Continued follow-up needed to determine if early advantage is maintained

## Clinical Implications and Guideline Impact


### Implications for Clinical Practice


**Major Breakthrough:**

1. PROCTOR is the first randomized study comparing native vessel PCI and SVG PCI in post-CABG patients
2. Provides high-quality evidence to guide clinical decisions
3. Challenges current guideline recommendations (IIa B)


**Applicable Patient Population:**

- Patients in whom both strategies are deemed technically feasible by multidisciplinary heart team
- Cohorts with high prevalence of complex native CTO lesions
- Selected patient population


**Cautious Application:**

1. Only 1-year follow-up data available
2. SVG disease progression may emerge during longer-term follow-up
3. Need to await 3-5 year long-term results


### Should Guidelines Change?


**Discussion by Dr. de Winter and Dr. Ahmad:**

**Current Recommendations:**
quote
*"We should remain cautious because this is a selected patient population, and SVG disease progression may not be fully apparent at 1-year follow-up. Results at 3 or 5 years may be completely different."*  

--- Ruben de Winter, MD
quote


- Maintain caution given only 1-year data
- Awaiting 3-5 year long-term follow-up results is critical
- Should not hastily change guidelines before long-term results confirmed


**Practical Application Recommendations:**

1. If SVG lesion is readily approachable
2. Facing very complex native CTO
3. This study provides important randomized data supporting SVG PCI choice
4. Can predict likely 1-year outcomes in each group


**Not Applicable (Excluded from Trial):**

- Repeatedly stented vein grafts
- Horrible aneurysmal degenerated vein grafts
- Thrombus-laden grafts (extremely high no-reflow risk, very low durability)


### Impact on the Field


**Immediate Impact:**

1. Provides first randomized evidence to guide revascularization strategy selection in post-CABG patients
2. Significantly advances the field
3. Changes clinical practice considerations
4. Provides high-quality evidence base


**Potential Game-Changer:**
quote
*"If 3-5 year follow-up confirms maintenance of early advantage, this could completely transform the management strategy for revascularization in post-CABG patients."*  

--- Yousif Ahmad, MBBS, PhD

- 83


**Technical Requirements:**

1. Ability to handle high J-CTO scores
2. Calcified lesion intervention techniques
3. Mastery of complex techniques (retrograde through bypass graft)
4. Requires very experienced interventional cardiologists


**Enrollment Challenges:**

1. All patients must undergo heart team assessment
2. Must have clear indication for percutaneous revascularization
3. Both lesions must be prospectively assessed as technically feasible for PCI
4. These strict criteria made enrollment more difficult


## Study Limitations


### Trial Design-Related Limitations


1. **Follow-up Duration:**      - Only 1-year follow-up data available - Need 3-5 year long-term results - SVG disease progression may emerge long-term
2. **Patient Selection:**      - Selected patient population - Excluded certain high-risk SVG features - Results may not apply to all post-CABG patients
3. **Complexity Differences:**      - Native vessel group had extremely high CTO prevalence (83 - SVG group excluded highest-risk features - Baseline complexity may not be entirely equivalent between groups

## Conclusions and Future Perspectives


### Key Messages from the PROCTOR Trial


1. ****Contrary to Expectations:**** SVG PCI superior to native vessel PCI at 1 year
2. ****First Randomized Evidence:**** Provides high-quality evidence to guide clinical decisions
3. ****Challenges Current Guidelines:**** Current IIa B recommendation needs reassessment
4. ****Applies to Selected Patients:**** Patients in whom both strategies are deemed feasible
5. ****Requires Long-term Follow-up:**** Awaiting 3-5 year results is critical


### Recommendations for Clinicians


**Current Best Practice:**

1. **Individualized Assessment:**      - Consider patient-specific anatomical features - Assess technical feasibility of both strategies - Balance short-term versus long-term risks
2. **SVG PCI Considerations:**      - If SVG lesion is relatively straightforward - Facing very complex native CTO - PROCTOR provides 1-year outcome data support - Avoid high-risk SVG features (aneurysm, heavy thrombus)
3. **Native Vessel PCI Considerations:**      - Theoretically potentially better long-term durability - Requires high technical expertise - Higher risk of PCI-related complications - Especially with CTO lesions
4. **Multidisciplinary Discussion:**      - Heart team assessment is critical - Discuss feasibility of both strategies - Consider possibility of redo CABG - Fully communicate risks and benefits with patients


### Future Research Directions


**PROCTOR Trial Continued Follow-up:**
quote
*"Continued follow-up of the PROCTOR trial is critical to determine whether the early clinical advantage is maintained or if SVG disease progression reverses the advantage long-term."*  

--- PROCTOR Research Team
quote

**Key Questions:**

- Comparison of MACE rates at 3 and 5 years
- SVG restenosis and reocclusion rates
- Long-term durability of native vessel PCI
- Subgroup analysis results


**New Research Focus:**

1. **Optimizing SVG PCI:**      - Novel drug-eluting stents - Application of embolic protection devices - Pharmacotherapy optimization
2. **Improving Native Vessel PCI:**      - Advances in CTO intervention techniques - Intravascular imaging guidance - Novel techniques for calcified lesion treatment
3. **Prevention Strategies:**      - Improving long-term SVG patency - Slowing native vessel disease progression - Optimizing secondary prevention


### Final Reflections


**Potential Paradigm Shift:**
quote
*"If long-term follow-up confirms maintenance of SVG PCI advantage, PROCTOR could be a game-changing study, redefining the standard of care for revascularization in post-CABG patients."*
quote

**Ongoing Challenges:**

- Management of post-CABG patients remains complex
- Continued research and evidence accumulation needed
- Technical advances may change the treatment landscape
- Individualized treatment strategies are critical


Despite the unexpected results from the PROCTOR trial, it provides us with the first high-quality randomized controlled trial evidence and indicates that future research requires long-term follow-up to truly determine the optimal treatment strategy.
